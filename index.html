04

Three Questions About Your Pricing

These are the most valuable things in this report. They're questions, not criticisms but they need answering before we write your pricing page, because we can't write it without them.

1

How many visits is $249?

Your tiers currently read "scheduled home inspections" with no number attached. Your competitor's entire rate card is built on frequency: weekly, twice a month, monthly.

This matters in two directions. A prospect comparing you can't understand what they're buying, and neither can we we can't write copy that defends a price without knowing the unit. And it's also your capacity model: weekly visits across twenty homes is eighty visits a month. That's a full-time job before a single concierge request comes in.

What we need A visit count for each tier. Even a draft.
2

Should property size change your price?

Right now it doesn't. A 6,000 sq ft lodge in Big Canoe and a 1,200 sq ft cabin both cost $249. Same money, several times the work more square footage, more systems, more time on site, more liability.

Your competitor prices on nothing but size. The national guidance says the same: larger and more complex homes take longer and carry more risk, and they should cost more. At ten to twenty clients, a few large homes on a flat rate is the kind of thing that quietly erodes a margin you can't spare yet.

Our recommendation Add a size dimension even a simple two-band split. It protects you and it's what the market already expects.
3

Is $30 an hour right?

Your competitor charges $45–50 for a single monthly inspection visit perhaps twenty to thirty minutes on site. You're charging $30 an hour for personalized concierge work, with unbillable drive time across Big Canoe to Blue Ridge terrain built into every job. IF this is intentional to pull clients away from competitor pricing ignore this and move on

Industry guidance is direct about this: price for profit, not to win the job. Calculate the true cost per visit drive time, fuel, insurance, software, your time then add margin.

Our read This is likely underpriced. Worth revisiting before launch rather than after you've set an anchor with your founding members.

To be clear about the direction of this

None of these say "you're too expensive." The research points the other way against national norms for the segment you're targeting, you're priced conservatively. The issue is structure, not level. Define the unit, add the size dimension, and your pricing becomes defensible rather than debatable.

The Technology Reality

You asked about a client portal. Here's what your competitors actually have and it changes the plan in your favour.

Nobody in this market built anything

Home Watch of North Georgia's "Client Login" links out to QRIDit an off-the-shelf home watch software product. Their "Make Payment" links to a QuickBooks page. Home Watcher Pros uses QRIDit as well.

Both of your competitors run the same third-party subscription. Neither has custom technology.

This means portal parity isn't a build it's a subscription. You can have a client portal, timestamped visit logs and inspection reporting at the same level as both incumbents, on day one, for a monthly fee. We'd recommend adding this to Phase 1 rather than deferring it.

And their website

Home Watch of North Georgia's site carries a 2015 copyright. Their blog has two posts both from 2015. The homepage still leads with "The Winters of 2014 and 2015" as its cautionary example. It's built on a page builder that's been effectively deprecated for years.

  • Their phone link is broken. The site displays one number and the click-to-call link dials a different one. Anyone tapping it on a phone reaches the wrong number.
  • Two different email addresses appear on the same page one in the header, another in the footer.
  • Their social sharing is broken. Any time someone shares their site to Facebook or a text message, it renders blank.
  • Their listed address is a PO Box, which can't be used for a Google Business Profile.

Where this leaves you

Ruth's relationships are real and a decade deep. Her digital presence has been on autopilot since 2015. Those are two different things, and only one of them is defended.

A modern site, real Google reviews, the same QRIDit tooling, and a claimed Google Business Profile puts you ahead of her online almost immediately. That's where the Florida-based owner searching from five hundred miles away finds someone — and it's not a fight over relationships.

The Review Opening

This is the fastest advantage available to you, and it costs nothing.

All three of your competitors run on testimonials they published on their own websites. We could not find independent Google or Yelp star ratings for any of them. Home Watcher Pros has a Yelp page with no visible reviews. Their Facebook page has 151 likes.

We should be straight about the limits here: an absence of results in our research isn't proof that none exist. But it does mean that nothing independently verifiable surfaced for any competitor in this market.

What that's worth

Your competitors have a decade of head start on reputation and effectively no head start on verifiable reputation. A handful of genuine Google reviews would make you the most independently-validated home watch business in North Georgia.

Google reviews also feed local search rankings and AI recommendations in a way a testimonials page never will. When someone asks ChatGPT for a home watch service in Big Canoe, reviews are part of what it draws on.

Our recommendation: build the review request into your service from client one. Not as a campaign later as part of delivery. After the first completed walk-through report, a simple ask. Target five genuine Google reviews before you have ten clients. Your founding members will be the most motivated advocates you'll ever have.

What Your Clients Are Actually Afraid Of

The most useful thing we found wasn't competitive. It was linguistic. Your competitor's testimonials show exactly what North Georgia second-home owners worry about in their own words.

  • The ladybugs. A client found thousands of them during a scheduled inspection, photographed and flagged with a suggested fix. Their relief wasn't about property damage at all it was about not being embarrassed in front of arriving guests. That's a short-term rental anxiety, and it maps exactly onto your guest-arrival prep.
  • The frozen pipes. A client bought winterization because their neighbor skipped it the year before, had pipes freeze, and paid dearly for it. The trigger wasn't their own disaster it was watching someone else's.
  • The storm. A Florida-based Big Canoe owner heard about severe Georgia storms and received confirmation their property was fine before they could even ask. Proactive contact during weather events may be the single highest-value moment in this entire business.

Two objections already answered publicly by them

  • Who has access to my house? Their FAQ names this fear directly that with some companies you don't know who holds your keys, alarm code and contact details then answers it: only bonded and insured team members. If your FAQ doesn't address this too, you look evasive by comparison. It's the biggest objection in your business.
  • The free neighbor. They frame the real alternative as a well-meaning neighbor who can't do this professionally and point out that when something serious goes wrong, your property becomes an imposition on that friendship. That's your actual competition, not the other companies. And it's a genuinely effective reframe that nobody's using on your behalf yet.

What we'll do with this

Your site gets written to these specific fears rather than to abstractions about peace of mind. Autumn winterization content aimed at Big Canoe owners. Proactive storm contact named explicitly as a tier benefit. And an FAQ that answers the access question before it's asked.

A Credential Worth Considering

The National Home Watch Association has been the industry's standards body since 2009. Two of your three competitors lean on it.

They accredit businesses after a formal vetting process background checks, verified insurance and bonding, business license verification, a code of ethics, and a consumer complaint search. Accreditation renews annually. Accredited members appear in a national directory that homeowners search directly, and can display the badge.

Home Watcher Pros advertises itself as North Georgia's only certified and accredited home watch service. That claim is doing real work for them.

Why it fits your business specifically

You're asking people to hand over gate codes and alarm information for a house they're not standing in. "I've been independently vetted and background-checked" is a far stronger answer than "trust me" particularly before you have reviews.

It's three things from one credential: a trust answer, a directory listing that isn't Google, and a third-party source that AI search tools can cite when someone asks for a recommendation.

The honest caveats

It requires verified insurance and bonding. That's the gate, and it has to come first.

We haven't confirmed their current fees, timeline, or whether Georgia adds any requirements on top. We'd rather tell you that than guess. A direct call to NHWA settles it we're happy to put together the questions to ask.

And it won't market you. Accreditation removes an objection; it doesn't create demand. It's a credibility floor, not a lead engine. Your site, your Google Business Profile and your first reviews still do the heavy lifting.

Where You Win

Three things this market doesn't have that you do.

One: the video walk-through

Both major competitors deliver checklist reports with photos through QRIDit. Neither advertises video. This is the one capability claim you can make that the market doesn't already answer.

It also means we lead with the video, not with "detailed reporting" reporting is table stakes here, and saying it doesn't distinguish you. Showing someone their own kitchen does.

Two: concierge as a published product

We should be precise here, because our first read was too generous. Concierge work does exist in this market Home Watcher Pros lists it among their specialties, and Home Watch of North Georgia runs a broad à la carte menu.

But nobody sells it as a clear, named, published-price membership. Theirs is bolted on as custom quotes and add-ons. Yours is the product itself. That's a narrower difference than "nobody does this" and a more defensible one, because it's about how you package and price rather than what you're capable of.

Three: the owner story

Peachy Home Watch's entire differentiation is who the owner is the personal history, the family, the decades in Georgia. It's proof this works in your market.

Your customer-service career and your "someone local you can trust" angle belong prominently on your About page, not buried beneath a services list. In a business where the product is trust, the person is the product.

Two Channels Worth Opening Early

Real estate agents

Home Watch of North Georgia runs a dedicated service line for agents visiting listings at a requested time to turn on lights and music and open the blinds before a showing, and providing before-and-after reports around storm events. They note that their agent clients advertise the service to win more listings.

That's a referral engine hiding inside a home watch business. Agents who use you for listings then recommend you to the buyers of those second homes. You listed real estate partnerships as a future item we'd argue it belongs closer to day one.

One caution worth naming: Ruth Leonard came out of that exact community. You'll need a reason for agents to choose you, and it probably isn't price.

Google Business Profile

The single highest-impact thing for local search, and it's free. Your direct competitor lists a PO Box, which isn't eligible as a Google Business Profile address meaning there may be an opening in the map results that nobody currently owns properly.

We'll set this up as part of Phase 1: categories, service area, photos, and a direct link back to the site.
